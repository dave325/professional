What is Stress?

Stress is perfectly normal. It is just a sense of danger - real or imaginary - that kicks your body's defenses into high gear, also known as "fight or flight" or the stress response. It is a big difference between being stressed and overly stresses. Stress has a positive effect on your body. It helps you stay energetic, focused, and alert. In certain situations it can save your life, and can sharpen your concentration and drives you to do what is more important. Being overly stressed can cause major damage to many areas such as:

  • Your productivity
  • Emotional state
  • Health and mood

College life and Stress

It is no surprise that college can be extremely stressful on an individual. College students are made to adjust to an independent life, learning to balance their social, work and academic life. College students are constantly given deadlines for assignments, projects, midterms, finals, etc.. Contrary to popular belief, stress is not only caused by negative life events such as strict deadlines, arguments etc.. but can be caused by positive events such as clubs, graduating etc..

Stress is brought about when you are adapting to any situation whether it is new or old

What are stress Responses

A common term many individuals either hear or use is "fight or flight". What exactly is the "fight or flight response?"

Answer

The human body sends a signal to the brain giving it a warning signal that you are in danger, "beginning with the release of the hormone ACTH into the bloodstream. Within seconds, there is the output of adrenalin and other stress-related hormones, a rise in blood pressure, an increase in glucose levels and the alerting of the major organs and sensory systems to prepare for physical action."

The first step of recognizing stress is understanding the three components of stress: Physiological, cognitive and behavioral.

Physiological Symptoms

Physiological symptoms of stress are the response of the body to stressful situations.
Some physiological signs of stress are: shallow, rapid breathing, heart racing, increased perspiration, etc..

Cognitive Symptoms

Cognitive Symptoms of stress is the way we mentally process our surroundings.
Some cognitive signs of stress are: difficulty concentrating, easily distracted, tendency to be negative, etc..

Behavioral Symptoms

Behavioral symptoms of stress are actions performed that are observed by other people. Some behavioral signs of stress are: irritability, sarcasm, losing patience, etc..

Handling Stress in healthy ways!

Being aware of stressful situations is the key component to managing stress. An effective way of monitoring and understanding where your stress comes from is by using a journal/diary. By keeping a record of your stressors you can have a better understanding of how you react to situations you are in.

5 tips complimentary of Union College Counseling Center

  1. Set realistic, clear goals and prioritize your time. Evaluate goals periodically, and have the flexibility to modify them when appropriate. Many college students take on too many activities without considering which ones are truly important and/or rewarding. If you find yourself overly stressed, consider letting go of some of your activities that you are only doing because you feel you "should."
  2. Deal with both success and failure with a balanced attitude. Recognize that disappointing/negative experiences are a natural part of the cycles of life. Use crises as opportunities for growth, and to reaffirm values and break unhealthy patterns.
  3. Don't drift along in troublesome or emotionally draining relationships or situations. Take steps to resolve the problems, or consider ending the relationship or experience.
  4. Make time to be with significant others in relationships that are trusting and reciprocal, and that leave you feeling energized.
  5. Find time every day for some form of relaxation or fun, such as watching your favorite show, exercising, laughing with a friend, or spending needed time alone.
